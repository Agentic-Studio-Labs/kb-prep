Financial Literacy

Grades 4–5

Lesson 2: How Can SMART Goals Help You Achieve Your Dreams?

Lesson 2

Overview

Lesson Description: In this lesson, students will explore the SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) framework to set and achieve effective goals. They will brainstorm personal and financial goals, applying SMART criteria to clarify and track their objectives. By using a goal tracker, students will monitor their progress and learn how consistent effort helps them achieve their dreams. This lesson links financial literacy with essential life skills, empowering students to set actionable and realistic goals.

Note to Teachers: Introducing the SMART goal framework for personal and financial goals equips students with transferable skills for various aspects of life. To help students grasp the concept, provide relatable examples such as improving in a sport, completing a project, or saving for something meaningful. Use engaging analogies and encourage peer discussions to make the concepts accessible. For students who may find goal-setting challenging, scaffold the process by breaking goals into small, manageable steps. The goal tracker is a valuable tool that fosters self-discipline and accountability, helping students build confidence in their ability to plan and achieve.

Time

The primary lesson activities are designed for a 45-minute session. The lesson also includes optional enrichment activities for deeper engagement and extended learning. Start with the ENGAGE activity, watch the Green Our Planet Studios video, and continue with the remaining sections in order. Feel free to adjust the content or sequence to suit your students' needs and the instructional goals. The activities can be taught in one session or spread over multiple sessions or days as needed.

Vocabulary

achievable: realistic and something you can actually do

goal: something you want to achieve, like saving up for a toy or improving in a sport

measurable: can be tracked with numbers to see how close you are to reaching it

relevant: connected to something important you want to accomplish

SMART goal: a well-defined plan that helps you reach a specific, achievable goal by making it measurable, relevant to you, and time-bound

specific: clearly explained so you know exactly what you’re trying to do

time-bound: has a deadline or a specific time by when it needs to be finished

Learning Objectives

Students will apply and demonstrate an understanding of the SMART goal framework by making each goal specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound.

Students will use a progress tracker to monitor their goals, reinforcing the importance of tracking and adjusting steps to achieve their objectives.

Essential Question

How can SMART goals help you achieve your dreams?

Cross-Curricular Connections:

Math: Students will calculate weekly or monthly savings goals and use basic arithmetic to assess timelines for achieving their SMART goals.

English Language Arts (ELA): Students will develop writing and critical thinking skills by articulating SMART goals and explaining how their steps lead to success.

Economics: Students will connect SMART goals to saving and budgeting, learning how financial planning supports long-term stability.

Social Studies: Students will explore how personal financial goals reflect societal values like resource management and community responsibility.

SEL Competencies:

Students will use self-awareness to reflect on their personal aspirations and strengths when developing SMART goals, fostering a deeper understanding of their values and priorities.

Students will demonstrate self-management by applying organizational skills to break down their goals into actionable steps and using a progress tracker to stay accountable.

Students will demonstrate social awareness by actively listening to and providing feedback on their peers' goals during group discussions, fostering empathy and collaborative problem-solving.

Students will use responsible decision-making to evaluate their goals using the SMART framework, analyzing the practicality and relevance of each step to ensure long-term success.

Family Engagement Strategies:

Building Family Support for SMART Goal Success: Encourage students to share their financial goals and progress tracking tools with a family member or trusted adult to foster collaboration and accountability. Use strategies such as sending home the SMART Goals Letter 4-5.FL.2 Handout A (SMART Goals at Home Letter) to explain the lesson and encourage family involvement, and implementing review and reflection sessions where students and caregivers can work together to engage students and caregivers in supporting goal achievement.

Send Home the SMART Goals Letter 4-5.FL.2 Handout A. SMART Goals at Home Letter. This will explain the lesson and encourage involvement.

Review and Reflect Together:

Students will bring home their 4-5.FL.2 Handout C. SMART Goal Progress Tracker and explain their goal to a family member or trusted adult.

Prompt them to discuss why their goal is meaningful and the steps they plan to take to achieve it.

Family members or trusted adults should be encouraged to ask reflective questions, such as:

What small steps are you taking this week to reach your goal?

What challenges do you think you might face, and how can we work through them together?

Household Progress Check-Ins: Suggest that students and caregivers set a regular time (e.g., once a week) to review the progress tracker. Encourage them to celebrate milestones together, such as reaching the halfway point or completing a specific measurable task.

Encourage Positive Reinforcement: Caregivers can provide verbal encouragement or small rewards when students demonstrate effort and progress toward their goals. Examples include a “goal tracker celebration” dinner or a family outing when a milestone is achieved.

Collaborative Goal-Setting Activity: Caregivers can join the student in creating a shared SMART goal. For instance, they could save together for an event, like a weekend outing or a new board game. Use this activity to show how teamwork and shared accountability make goals more achievable.

Discussion Prompts for Households: Provide students with conversation starters to guide their discussions at home. Examples:

What are some goals you’ve worked toward as a family or household, and how did you achieve them?

What advice would you give me about staying focused on my goal?

Creating a Supportive Environment: Encourage caregivers to share their own goals and progress with the student. This exchange can build mutual accountability and show students the value of goal-setting as a lifelong skill.

Procedures

ENGAGE: Opening Activity (10 minutes)

Goal Setting Adventure: Explain: Today, we’re going on a Goal Adventure! Every adventurer has dreams or things they want to achieve, like earning a special prize, finishing a big project, or learning a new skill. We’re going to talk about some of our dreams and what it takes to turn them into real-life achievements!

Dream Big—Brainstorming Goals Together: Invite students to close their eyes for a moment and think of one big dream for the future and one smaller dream they’d like to reach soon, like this school year. Give some fun examples. You can say: Maybe you dream of becoming an artist or traveling somewhere exciting one day. Or maybe you want to score a goal in soccer this season or finish reading a big chapter book. Then, let students share one of their goals with a partner, or, if time allows, with the class.

Goals as a Treasure Map: Explain: To reach a goal, we need to make a plan—like following a treasure map! Just like on an adventure, we take small steps toward our goals. Setting goals is a way to mark out the path. Use a simple visual. Draw a treasure map on the board with a starting point, steps along the path, and a treasure chest at the end. Label the map with “steps” toward a goal.

Class Example of a Goal Map: Choose a fun class goal and plot it on the treasure map together. For example, you could say: Our class wants to read 5 books by the end of the year or raise money for our school garden or class trip. Ask: What small steps could help us get there? Students might suggest reading a little each day, tracking books, or doing a monthly book tally. Add these ideas as steps on the treasure map.

Preparing to Make Our Goals SMART: Explain: A great way to follow our map is by making our goals clear and easy to track. We’ll start thinking about how to make each goal clear and simple, so we can see our progress. In our adventure, we’ll learn how to make our goals even stronger.

EXPLORE: Group Discussion and Activities (10 minutes)

Goal Types and SMART Mapping: Explain to students: Goals can be short-term (something you want to accomplish soon, today or this week) or long-term (something that takes longer, like during a semester or a big dream like a future career goal). Short-term and long-term goals will be covered in more detail in Lesson 5. Prompt students: Think about both types of goals and why having a mix of short-term and long-term goals can help us achieve our dreams.

Class Example Goal Mapping: Next, choose one class goal that is achievable and relatable, like reading more books as a class. As a group, begin breaking it down using the SMART criteria. The full breakdown will be done in the EXPLAIN section.

Class Discussion and Reflection: These questions will guide students to connect with the SMART criteria and understand the benefits of creating specific, measurable, and realistic goals. Ask students:

Why might this goal need to be specific?

How would a measurable component help us know we’re on track?

What makes a goal achievable?

What makes one realistic vs. unrealistic?

Why does having a timeline help us stay focused?

EXPLAIN: Concepts Explained (15 minutes)

Create an Anchor Chart: Create an anchor chart with students on chart paper to reinforce the concepts of SMART goal setting

Use 4-5.FL.2 Anchor Chart. SMART Goals and Achieving Your Dreams as a guideline/example.

An effective anchor chart should be created together as a class and then displayed in the classroom throughout the unit for students to refer back to.

Implement the anchor chart at the beginning of the section to introduce key vocabulary or at the end to reinforce the vocabulary covered during the lesson.

Reinforcing Key Vocabulary: In this section, you will reinforce the lesson vocabulary introduced in the Green Our Planet Studios video. If teaching this lesson over multiple days, students may need more instruction to reinforce the previously introduced terms and concepts. Reiterate the concept of SMART goals by explaining that these are goals that are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. Briefly define each part:

Specific: Clearly define what you want to achieve.

Measurable: Make sure the goal can be tracked with numbers or progress.

Achievable: The goal should be realistic.

Relevant: The goal must be important to your life.

Time-bound: Set a deadline to achieve it.

Class Discussion: Have students pick one example goal that they discussed earlier (personal or classroom goal) and, as a group, try to transform that goal into a SMART goal using the defined criteria.

Focus on Measurable Goals: Transition the discussion to focus specifically on the Measurable aspect of SMART goals. Explain that for a goal to be measurable, it needs a numerical component—something that can be tracked. For example:

Studying for a specific number of minutes.

Attending a certain number of tutoring sessions.

Completing a set number of practice problems.

Complete Part 1 of Handout A: Pass out the 4-5.FL.2 Handout B. SMART Goal Brainstorm and Planning, and read “Part 1: Two Goals” to the class. Students may use goals they created in the ENGAGE or ELABORATE activity, but do not have to. In this section, they will create the basic goal. In the remaining assignment, they will transform their goal into a SMART goal. Read aloud the instructions and example of “Part 2: SMART Goals” of 4-5.FL.2 Handout B. SMART Goal Brainstorm and Planning. Instruct students to work with a partner or group of three to complete this step. Each student will complete their own handout while helping one another brainstorm ways to turn their goal into a SMART goal. As students are working, circulate the room to help them.

Discuss Their SMART Goals: As students complete their SMART goals, instruct them to come to the board and write one of their SMART goals. By the end of the lesson, there should be a collection of SMART goals written. Spend a few minutes looking at the SMART goals as a class and discussing them as time allows. Point out components of each goal that fit the criteria of SMART goals.

ELABORATE: Application of Knowledge (10 minutes)

Goal Progress and Tracking Handout: Conclude the lesson by reviewing with students the 4-5.FL.2 Handout C. SMART Goal Progress Tracker. Encourage students to reflect on small steps they can take toward achieving their goals.

Class Discussion: Ask students to share one action they will take this week to make progress toward their SMART goals. Discuss how taking small, measurable steps can lead to long-term success.

Personal Progress Tracking: Encourage students to begin completing their progress tracker today for one of their goals as they take steps to achieve their goals. Review the progress periodically. A suggested timeline is to set aside 20 minutes as a class to review, reflect, and share progress once per week.

Reflection: End the Elaborate section by gathering the class for a brief reflective discussion. Ask:

What is one way having a SMART goal will help you succeed?

How does tracking your progress make it easier to stay focused?

Why is it helpful to break big goals into small steps?

Exit Ticket/Journal Activity: To wrap up the lesson, have students complete a journal entry or exit ticket reflecting on how the SMART goal framework helps them work toward their dreams. They should briefly describe their goal, steps they’ll take to achieve it, and any challenges they anticipate. Encourage them to think about strategies for staying on track, like asking for help or adjusting their plan, and to consider how tracking their progress will support their success. This reflection helps reinforce the importance of setting clear, achievable goals and taking specific steps to reach them.

Evaluation: Use the 4-5.FL.2 Rubric for Handout B. SMART Goal Brainstorm and Planning to assess the final SMART goal plan. The rubric will evaluate the goal’s clarity, the feasibility of steps, and the organization and accuracy of the timeline.

Steps they would take to create a SMART Goal.

ELABORATE FURTHER/REFLECT OR EXTEND: Enrichment (15 minutes)

Goal Ladder Activity: The goal here is to help students break down their long-term goals into manageable, actionable steps using a visual representation. Say to students: Sometimes big goals can feel overwhelming, but breaking them down into smaller steps makes them easier to achieve. Today, we’ll create a Goal Ladder to help you visualize the steps needed to reach your dreams!

Materials Needed:

Rulers (optional, to create straight ladder lines)

Markers, crayons, or colored pencils

Sample Goal Ladder for demonstration

Steps for Students:

Draw the Ladder: Students will draw a simple ladder with several rungs on their paper. The number of rungs depends on how many steps they believe are necessary to reach their goal.

Top Rung—Write the Goal: Instruct students to think about a meaningful long-term goal and write it at the very top of their ladder. Example: Become a veterinarian.

Work Backward—Identify Steps: Starting just below the goal, write the final step needed to achieve it. For example: Complete veterinary school. Continue working backward down the ladder, identifying the prior steps necessary to reach the next rung. Example for "Veterinarian":

Complete veterinary school.

Graduate from college with a degree in biology or animal science.

Get good grades in high school and volunteer at an animal shelter.

Study hard and learn about animals by reading books or watching videos.

Add Details (Optional):

Students can add a timeline for each step (e.g., "By age 18: Graduate high school").

Encourage creativity by adding illustrations or symbols next to each step.

Classroom Sharing and Reflection: After completing their Goal Ladders, have students share their ladders with a partner or small group. Facilitate a discussion:

What was the most exciting part of creating your ladder?

What steps do you think will be the most challenging?

How can you ask for help or stay motivated to keep climbing your ladder?

Teacher Reflection Prompt: Encourage students to keep their Goal Ladders somewhere visible, like in a folder or at home, to remind them of the steps they’re taking toward their dreams. Say to students: Remember, each small step brings you closer to your goal!

Accommodations and Differentiation

Provide sentence starters to help students struggling with articulating their SMART goals.

Pair English language learners with peers to support vocabulary and goal-setting discussions.

Offer pre-filled SMART goal templates for students needing additional guidance.

Allow students to use speech-to-text tools or draw visual representations of their goals instead of writing.

Create small groups for collaborative brainstorming to support students who benefit from peer interaction.

STEM-related Independent Practice

SMART Goals for Tracking Environmental Impact: The goal for this activity is to teach students how to use the SMART goal framework to address environmental conservation issues, linking financial literacy concepts with sustainability. Say to students: We can all make a difference when it comes to protecting our environment! Today, you’ll create a SMART goal to help conserve resources or protect the planet. You’ll then track your progress and see how small changes can have a big impact.

Paper or digital SMART goal templates

Markers or colored pencils (for illustrations)

Graph paper or access to a spreadsheet (for tracking)

Sample SMART goal example to show students

Brainstorm Environmental Goals: Ask students to think about problems in their community or at home related to environmental conservation. Provide examples to spark ideas:

Reducing plastic use by bringing reusable bags to the store.

Saving water by turning off the faucet while brushing teeth.

Recycling more by setting up a recycling bin at home.

Planting trees or starting a small garden.

Refine the Goal Using SMART Criteria: Review the SMART goal framework. Guide students in refining their goals using the SMART framework. Example:

Specific: Reduce the use of plastic water bottles by using a reusable bottle.

Measurable: Avoid using 20 plastic bottles in one month.

Achievable: Replace one disposable bottle each day.

Relevant: Protect the environment by reducing plastic waste.

Time-bound: Complete this goal within one month.

Create a Tracking System: Students will design a system to monitor their progress. Options include:

A journal to log daily efforts (e.g., "Used a reusable bottle today").

A chart or table to track specific actions (e.g., "Number of bottles avoided: 3").

A bar graph or line chart to visualize progress over time.

Digital tools like a spreadsheet or goal-tracking app.

Personalize Their Plan: Encourage students to include visuals, like drawings or stickers, to make their tracking system fun and engaging. Add milestone rewards, such as a small treat or acknowledgment for reaching halfway points.

Classroom Sharing and Reflection: Have students share their SMART goals with the class or in small groups. Facilitate a discussion:

What inspired your goal?

What challenges might you face, and how can you overcome them?

How will you celebrate when you achieve your goal?

Teacher Reflection Prompt: Encourage students to think about how their actions impact their community and the environment. Say to students: Even small steps can lead to big changes. By tracking your progress, you’ll see how every action counts toward making the planet a better place!
